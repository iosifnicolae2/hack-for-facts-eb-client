# The Seven Major Red Flags

> **Key Insight:** Each red flag has three parts: **what it looks like**, **why it's a problem**, and **what questions to ask**.

## The Seven Red Flags in Detail

### ðŸš© Red Flag #1: Low Execution Rates

**What It Looks Like:**
- Investment (Code 71 - Cheltuieli de capital) execution significantly below allocated budget
- Money allocated but not spent
- Especially common for capital projects

**Why It's a Problem:**
- Infrastructure promised but not delivered
- Schools, hospitals, roads not built
- EU funds at risk of being lost

> **Legal Context:** Romanian law contains no fixed percentage threshold for acceptable execution rates. However, **OUG 90/2023** establishes that if execution falls below **50% by October 31**, credit orderers must transfer minimum 10% of remaining credits to the Government Reserve Fund. Court of Accounts audits typically observe 70-90% execution rates as a practical benchmark.

**Questions to Ask:**
- "Why was only X% of the investment budget spent?"
- "Which projects were cancelled or delayed?"
- "Will the money be reallocated or lost?"

> **Example:** City X planned 50M RON for road repairs. Actual spending: 22M RON (44%). Result: Only 3 of 7 planned roads were repaired.

---

### ðŸš© Red Flag #2: Frequent Rectifications

**What It Looks Like:**
- Multiple budget amendments in a single year with large changes
- Significant shifts (>15% of original budget)
- Changes timed near elections

**Why It's a Problem:**
- Poor initial planning
- Political manipulation of priorities
- Unreliable budget as policy tool

> **Legal Context (Law 273/2006, Art. 19):** There is **no legal limit** on how many rectifications are permitted per year. Rectifications may be approved within 30 days of state budget rectification, based on substantiated proposals. However, **budget balance must be maintained** and **deliberative authority (local council) approval** is required for all rectifications. While legal, frequent large changes suggest planning issues.

**Questions to Ask:**
- "Why was the original budget so inaccurate?"
- "Who benefited from each rectification?"
- "Were any cuts made to fund increases elsewhere?"

---

### ðŸš© Red Flag #3: Arrears (Arierate)

**What It Looks Like:**
- Unpaid bills accumulating
- "Arrears" appearing in execution reports
- Growing debt to suppliers

**Why It's a Problem:**
- Institution is spending beyond its means
- Suppliers may stop providing services
- Hidden debt that must eventually be paid

> **Legal Definition (Law 273/2006, Art. 2(4Â¹)):** Arrears are outstanding payments with maturity of more than **90 days** from the due date. Credit orderers with arrears must **reduce their volume monthly by at least 5%**. Non-compliance triggers **automatic suspension** of defalcated quotas and sums from state budget revenues. Units with arrears **cannot authorize new loans** until cleared.

**Questions to Ask:**
- "What is the current arrears balance?"
- "How long have these bills been unpaid (90+ days = arierate)?"
- "Is there a plan to achieve the required 5% monthly reduction?"

---

### ðŸš© Red Flag #4: Personnel Crowding Out Investment

**What It Looks Like:**
- Personnel costs consuming a disproportionate share of total budget
- Investment consistently \<10%
- Salary execution 99%+, investment much lower

**Why It's a Problem:**
- No money for development
- Institution maintaining status quo only
- Infrastructure deteriorating

> **Legal Context:** Romanian regulations use **maximum position limits** (OUG 63/2010) rather than percentage thresholds for personnel costs. Court of Accounts data shows personnel costs average ~46% for commune/town administrations. Only **31% of communes** could cover personnel costs from own revenues in 2023. While no legal percentage exists, high personnel-to-investment ratios remain a practical concern for development capacity.

**Questions to Ask:**
- "What is the personnel-to-investment ratio?"
- "Has staffing increased while investment decreased?"
- "What development projects are being sacrificed?"

---

**What It Looks Like:**
- Multiple contracts just under the direct purchase threshold
- Same supplier, similar dates, similar services
- High percentage of direct awards

**Why It's a Problem:**

- Often leads to higher prices
- May indicate favoritism or kickbacks

### ðŸš© Red Flag #6: Emergency Procurement Abuse

**What It Looks Like:**
- High percentage of "emergency" procedures (negociere fÄƒrÄƒ publicare prealabilÄƒ)
- Non-emergency items procured urgently
- Same suppliers winning repeatedly

**Why It's a Problem:**
- Bypasses competitive procedures
- Often results in higher prices
- May indicate collusion or corruption

> **Legal Requirements (Law 98/2016, Art. 104(1)(c)):** Emergency procurement requires **three cumulative conditions**:
> 1. **Extreme urgency** exists
> 2. Urgency is caused by **unforeseeable events**
> 3. Situation is **not attributable to any action or inaction** of the contracting authority
>
> These conditions are interpreted **strictly** by courts. Non-notification penalties: 5,000-30,000 lei fine.

**Questions to Ask:**
- "What was the emergency that justified this? Were all three legal conditions met?"
- "Why wasn't this planned in advance (was inaction a factor)?"
- "How does this price compare to market rates?"

---

### ðŸš© Red Flag #7: Opaque Transfers

**What It Looks Like:**
- Large amounts in "Other transfers" categories
- Money moving between entities without clear purpose
- Transfers to organizations with unclear mandates

**Why It's a Problem:**
- Hides true spending purposes
- May fund activities outside normal oversight
- Creates opportunities for misuse

> **Budget Classification (Order MFP 1954/2005):**
> - **Title VI (Code 51):** Transfers between government units - subject to explicit legal frameworks
> - **Title VII (Code 55):** Other transfers (Alte transferuri) - less specific oversight
>
> Code 55 includes internal transfers (55.01) like housing support, state aid schemes, and de minimis aid. These require deliberative authority approval but have less rigorous oversight than inter-governmental transfers.

**Questions to Ask:**
- "What specific activities do these transfers fund?"
- "Who are the ultimate recipients?"
- "What outcomes were achieved?"
