# From Anomaly to Story

## Concept Introduction

Finding something unusual in budget data is just the beginning. An anomaly is not a story â€” it's a lead. The journey from "this looks odd" to "we can publish this" requires systematic verification, pattern recognition, and multiple sources.

Too many would-be investigations fail because journalists confuse the starting point with the finish line. The investigation funnel keeps you honest about where you actually are.

> **Key Insight:** Anomalies are LEADS, not conclusions. Never publish an anomaly without verification and context.

### The Verification Triangle

To move from anomaly to story, you need three types of evidence:

| Evidence Type | What It Provides | Examples |
|---------------|------------------|----------|
| **Data** | The pattern | Budget execution rates, contract values, spending trends |
| **Documents** | The mechanism | Meeting minutes, procurement files, internal memos |
| **Sources** | The context | Officials who explain why, whistleblowers who confirm |

A complete story requires all three. Data alone is suggestive. Documents alone are incomplete. Sources alone are unverified. Together, they're publishable.

### Common Traps to Avoid

#### Trap 1: Publishing Anomalies as Stories

**Bad:** "Ministry X spent only 40% of infrastructure budget!"

**Problem:** No context. Maybe projects were delayed due to legitimate permits. Maybe funding was reallocated by Parliament. The anomaly is real; the implication is unverified.

**Better:** Investigate why, then report the verified finding.

#### Trap 2: Confusing Correlation with Causation

**Bad:** "Minister's brother got contract right after minister took office!"

**Problem:** Timing suggests connection but doesn't prove it. The brother might be a legitimate contractor who won on merit.

**Better:** Show the process was manipulated, not just that the outcome looks suspicious.

#### Trap 3: Single-Source Stories

**Bad:** "According to our source, there was bribery..."

**Problem:** One source can be wrong, biased, or have personal motives.

**Better:** Verify with documents and at least one additional independent source.
